Bloom's Revised Taxonomy

Originally developed in 1956 by Benjamin Bloom, revised in 2001 by Anderson & Krathwohl. A hierarchical cognitive framework describing six levels of intellectual engagement — from basic recall to complex creation. Essential for writing measurable learning outcomes.

  • Remember — Recall facts and concepts
  • Understand — Explain ideas
  • Apply — Use in new situations
  • Analyze — Draw connections
  • Evaluate — Justify decisions
  • Create — Produce original work

The ACM CCECC "Bloom's for Computing" initiative extended the standard verb list with computing-specific action verbs across all six levels.

Fink's Significant Learning

L. Dee Fink's 2003 framework from Creating Significant Learning Experiences. Unlike Bloom's hierarchy, Fink's six dimensions form an interactive web — each strengthens the others. Addresses the full scope of human learning, not just cognition.

  • FK — Foundational Knowledge
  • AP — Application
  • IN — Integration
  • HD — Human Dimension
  • CA — Caring
  • LL — Learning How to Learn

In the AI era, HD, CA, and LL dimensions are most resistant to substitution — they require authentic human experience and reflection.

Why Both Together?

Bloom's answers: Is this task cognitively rigorous enough? Fink's answers: Will students actually care and grow?

Assessments that score high on Bloom's (Create level) but only touch FK and AP in Fink's terms are still vulnerable to AI substitution. The strongest assignments activate multiple Fink dimensions — especially HD, CA, and LL.

The AI Audit: For each major assignment, ask: which Fink dimensions does this genuinely require? If only FK and AP — you've found your highest-risk assignment.
